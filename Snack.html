    • Top 10 Snacks in Malaysia


    1. Keropok Lekor


    TKeropok lekor is a traditional Malaysian snack that hails from the northeastern states of Terengganu and Kelantan, where it's considered a local delicacy. It's a type of fish cracker made primarily from fish paste and sago flour. The fish used in keropok lekor is usually mackerel or sometimes a combination of mackerel and other types of fish.





    2. Apam Balik


    You haven’t truly experienced Malaysian food until you thrill your taste buds with this sweet treat. A pancake-style snack wedded with the compact package of an omelet, apam balik is stuffed with more than a sufficient amount of sugar, peanuts and the occasional sprinkle of corn – it’s a dish that’s constantly being reinvented.





    3. Karipap


    Karipap, also known as curry puff, is a popular snack in Malaysia and other parts of Southeast Asia. It consists of a crispy pastry shell filled with a savory mixture, typically made with potatoes, onions, carrots, and minced meat (usually chicken or beef), seasoned with curry powder and other spices.





    4. Murukku


    Murukku is a popular South Indian snack that is also enjoyed in Malaysia and other parts of Southeast Asia, particularly among the Indian community. It is a crunchy and savory deep-fried snack made primarily from rice flour and urad dal (black gram dal), along with spices and seasonings.





    5. Iced Gem Biscuits


    Ice Gem biscuits are a type of colorful and sweet biscuit that is popular in Malaysia and other parts of Southeast Asia. They are characterized by their round shape, slightly crunchy texture, and brightly colored icing on top.





    6. Bahulu


    Bahulu is a traditional Malay sponge cake or cookie that is popular in Malaysia, Indonesia, and other parts of Southeast Asia. It is typically enjoyed as a snack or dessert, especially during festive occasions such as Hari Raya (Eid al-Fitr) and Chinese New Year. Bahulu is known for its light and fluffy texture, with a slightly crisp outer layer. It is made from a simple batter consisting of flour, eggs, sugar, and sometimes coconut milk or pandan flavoring. The ingredients are mixed together to form a smooth batter, which is then poured into special Bahulu molds.





    7. Ramly Burger


    Ramly Burger is a popular Malaysian street food burger that has become a cultural icon in Malaysia. A Ramly Burger typically consists of a beef or chicken patty (or sometimes a combination of both), seasoned with spices and flavorings, such as soy sauce and pepper. The patty is often wrapped in an egg omelette, which adds an extra layer of flavor and texture. Other common ingredients include lettuce, onions, mayonnaise, chili sauce, and sometimes cheese. One of the distinctive features of a Ramly Burger is the way it is prepared. The patty is often flattened and grilled on a flat-top griddle, allowing it to cook quickly and evenly. The egg omelette is then wrapped around the patty as it cooks, creating a flavorful casing.





    8. Goreng Pisang


    The popular Malay snack of goreng pisang (banana fritters) is one of those dishes that has variations in banana-growing countries around the world. The deep-frying helps caramelize the natural sugars in the bananas, making them even sweeter than they were to begin with. Some of Malaysia’s Chinese versions have unusually delicate and puffy batter.





    9. Cucur Udang


    Cucur Udang, also known as "prawn fritters" or "prawn crackers," is a popular Malaysian street food snack. It consists of whole prawns coated in a seasoned batter, skewered onto bamboo sticks, and deep-fried until crispy and golden brown.





    10. Nyonya Kuih


    Nyonya kuih, also known as Peranakan kuih or simply kuih, refers to a variety of traditional Malaysian and Singaporean desserts that originate from the Peranakan (Straits Chinese) culture. These bite-sized snacks or desserts are typically made from ingredients such as rice flour, coconut milk, pandan leaves, and palm sugar, resulting in a wide array of colorful and flavorful treats. Nyonya kuih comes in numerous shapes, colors, and textures, reflecting the rich culinary heritage of the Peranakan community.
